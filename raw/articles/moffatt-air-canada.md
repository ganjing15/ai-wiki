## Citation
Moffatt v Air Canada, 2024 BCCRT 149 — issued 2024-02-14 by the British Columbia Civil Resolution Tribunal.

## Facts (verbatim summary)
On 2022-11-11, Jake Moffatt visited Air Canada's website to book a flight following a death in the family. The chatbot told him he could "retroactively apply the bereavement fare rate to his regularly priced airline ticket" after travel.

"Contrary to the chatbot's advice, the hyperlinked webpage indicated that Air Canada's bereavement policy does not apply to requests for bereavement consideration after travel has been completed."

## Tribunal ruling (verbatim)
- Air Canada argued "the chatbot was a separate legal entity responsible for its own actions"
- Tribunal: Air Canada "still bore responsibility for all the information on its website, whether it came from a static page or a chatbot"
- Held: "Air Canada owed Mr. Moffatt a duty of care" given the "commercial relationship as a service provider and consumer"
- Found chatbot gave inaccurate information; Air Canada liable for negligent misrepresentation

## Damages
$650.88 (the difference between the bereavement fare and paid fare).

## Significance
First widely-cited common-law decision establishing that companies remain liable for AI chatbot responses on their own websites. Has been cited in subsequent commercial-AI risk discussions and is treated as an early authority on chatbot liability.
